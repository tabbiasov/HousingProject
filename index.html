Introduction

Housing costs have increased significantly throughout New York City since 1990. With the increasing rent burdens for low-and-moderate-income renters, inviting calls for more affordable housing. Mayor Bill de Blasio launched Housing New York plan in 2014, aimed to build and preserve 200,000 affordable housing units over the coming decades.

Affordable housing boom in South Bronx


Although market-rate housing is scarce in Bronx, the affordable housing is rising in Bronx which targets at people who cannot pay market rates. More than 5,000 affordable apartments are being developed or planned recently with different kinds of motivations from the city. The developers are competing for sites in the South Bronx and the land prices keep climbing. There is a huge affordable housing project get approved by City Council known as La Central. This project plans to bring 992 apartments to the neighborhood spread out over five buildings.

$1.4B revitalization plan brings more affordable housing to Central Brooklyn


Governor Cuomo has set aside $563 million for 3,000 units of affordable housing on six state-owned sites within these neighborhoods. The massive investment covers an expansive list of priorities for the neighborhoods of Crown Heights, East New York, Bedford-Stuyvesant, and Brownsville. People in these areas feared the gentrification caused by the investment. But the governor’s counsel, Alphonso David said “This plan counters gentrification, largely because of the housing component, and the jobs component.”

Affordable Housing vs. Gentrification in East Harlem


Rezoning has faced resistance in neighborhoods like East Harlem. The mayor’s strategy is building a more affordable New York through greater height and density. However, a great opposition from lots of people who don’t want any changes in their neighborhoods and who don’t want affordable housing and who want only affordable housing. The opponents thought mandatory zoning would create far too few apartments to meet the need. And for them, the construction means disruption, which inevitably means gentrification and dislocation.
